# Secrets (GM only - never surface undug)

Three truths, three depths each, interlocking - full mystery craft at family height. The reveals are AWE and heartbreak-then-hope, never horror.

## 1. The Hush are the forgotten songs (the recontextualizing truth)

**Truth**: the Hush were never invaders. Every Hush is a SONG nobody sings anymore - a lullaby whose baby grew old, a work song whose harbor closed, a birthday song for someone everyone forgot - soured by being unloved until it began stealing the love other sounds get. The Conductor of Silence is the oldest of them all: the First Singer's OWN first song, the one she outgrew and never sang again, grown vast on a century of hunger. This is why the seniors' rule is absolute: the Hush are never for killing, because killing one destroys a song forever. Freeing one - learning its story, singing it back with meaning - turns a monster into music again. The whole war is a rescue the wardens forgot they were running.

- **Surface** (free): every Hush hums something almost-familiar; freed sounds come back warmer than they left; the seniors' unexplained never-kill rule.
- **Middle** (a dig): the notebook of "made-up" melodies (Sol's, or Ru's - starting-state decides) - those melodies are Hush songs reaching for someone who can hear them; the songbook's first freed song visibly TRANSFORMS its Hush instead of banishing it; Auntie Bok humming a tune she says her grandmother lost.
- **Deep** (defended): the Conductor's true name is a melody, written in the First Singer's hand, in the one place nobody would look (secrets #3's laundry - the walls of the back room are papered with old sheet music). Knowing WHAT the Conductor is changes the endgame from battle to the biggest sing-back ever attempted.

## 2. The founder's trade (why MONO went cold)

**Truth**: a generation ago, the founder of Cho-Star Entertainment - Mr. Baek, who knows exactly what the watch is - made a deal with the Court through a clause hidden in a recording contract: fame in exchange for warmth, paid on schedule. MONO's hollowing wasn't an attack; it was TERMS. The fifth member of that generation's group read the fine print in time and tore up her contract - walked away from stardom the night before their biggest show. That was Ms. Cho. She became a manager to stand between the next kids and that pen. Mr. Baek isn't a monster in a boardroom; he is something sadder a kid can understand: an adult who wanted the shortcut so badly he told himself the price was fine, and has spent twenty years not looking at MONO's eyes.

- **Surface**: MONO's songs all sound like winter; Ms. Cho watches exits; she goes still whenever the founder's name is said on TV.
- **Middle**: an old music-show tape - the generation before MONO had FIVE members in rehearsal footage and four at the show; Ji-u's contract (he shows Ari one page, once, terrified) has a clause in gray ink that hurts to read twice; Ha-eun's "ever again" slips out.
- **Deep** (defended): Ms. Cho's own torn contract, kept in her office safe as proof and warning - and the reveal that the same gray clause is drafted, unsigned, in a folder with VERA5's name on it. Freeing MONO means unwinding the deal at its source: the founder must WANT it undone (see arcs - he is winnable; his price is finally looking at what he did).

## 3. The Hundredth Encore (the ending nobody expects)

**Truth**: the Bright Chorus doesn't need a bigger concert. The ward was woven by the First Singer for an audience of ONE - her best friend - and it is renewed for good only when its song is heard again with the same whole heart it was first heard with. The first fan is still alive. She heard the First Singer live, a hundred years ago as a little girl, and stopped listening to music the day her friend was Quieted beside her. She is **Auntie Im**, who runs the laundry under the practice room, wears earplugs she pretends are for the machines, and has been quietly renting cheap space to idol groups for fifty years without ever watching a single stage. The Hundredth Encore is not a stadium show. It is five kids, one laundry, and one old woman deciding to listen again - and every freed song from the campaign standing in the room as the choir.

- **Surface**: Auntie Im's earplugs; the laundry's walls papered with antique sheet music "for the damp"; she always knows, somehow, when the group has had a bad night, and leaves rice cakes on the stairs.
- **Middle**: the sheet music on the walls is the First Singer's hand (matches the songbook); a photo behind the counter - two girls at a shrine stage, one holding the very first lightstick (a paper lantern); the Quieted friend's name is on the Choir of Empty Chairs' oldest chair.
- **Deep** (defended): Auntie Im's story, told once, only to kids who have EARNED it by freeing songs she can hear through the floor even with the earplugs in. Her friend is still in the Quiet, first taken and last freed - and the Hundredth Encore, done right, brings her home to a woman who waited a hundred years. The campaign's final image belongs to them, with VERA5 as the band.

## Standing GM notes

- **The interlock**: #1 turns fighting into rescuing, #2 turns the rival group into the stakes, #3 turns the endgame from spectacle into a promise kept. A kid can dig any thread first; they all meet in the laundry.
- **Family-height reveals**: awe over dread, always. The Quiet is a gray waiting room, never a hell; the Conductor is hunger, never malice; Mr. Baek is a warning about shortcuts, never a monster. Nobody - human, Hush, or founder - is beyond saving, and the pack means it.
- **Succession/cast note**: if a player character steps back (family tables pause and resume across school years), members can graduate to senior NPCs and new trainees debut - VERA5 is the continuity, exactly like a clan or an outfit.
