# The crime economy (GM reference)

Every trade in Calder Bay, what it takes to enter, what it pays, and what it costs. Treat these as the world's PHYSICS, not a menu to read aloud: the player discovers them through people, prices, and consequences. Every stream the player enters becomes an `Operations.md` row; every rival's version already exists in their GM file. Earnings are honest ranges - the dice, the crew, and the fiction move them.

**Proportions (era truth, hold this line)**: BOOZE IS THE ECONOMY - roughly seven of every ten criminal dollars in Calder Bay are the lake, the stills, and the rooms that pour. Gambling and the rackets split most of the rest. Dope is a MARGIN trade, small and shameful in 1926, which is exactly why nobody looks at who owns it (secrets.md #1). A rival's ledger, a hijacking's value, a war's stakes - price them all against booze first.

## Booze - the vertical

The trade the era is named for. Entry at any rung; empires integrate upward:

- **Buying wholesale** (anyone): cases from a connected middleman at triple Canadian price. No route, no margin - a corner business.
- **The run** (boat/trucks + nerve): buy legal at Port Sarnelle, cross the water. A boat run clears $800-2,000; winter ice roads are cheaper and meaner. Risks: hijack (the Carricks tithe the water), the Bureau's photographs, the lake itself. The ROUTE is the asset; the Fontaines' respect is the license.
- **Production** (stills, near-beer plants): moonshine stills in drug stores and plumbers' backrooms ($200-400/wk each, fire risk, blindings if cut greedy); real beer needs a BREWERY, and Brauer owns those.
- **Alky cooking** (the cottage network): pay Flats families to run kitchen stills - $15/wk per household for their gallons, hundreds of households at scale. Cheap, decentralized, raid-proof by dispersion; the costs are human (kitchen fires, sick kids, a neighborhood that becomes your factory and knows it). The outfit that runs the Flats this way OWNS the Flats - its votes, its silence, and its resentments.
- **The cutting plant** (chemist + secrecy): stretch good whiskey 3:1 with grain alcohol, caramel, and nerve. Doubles margin; a bad batch kills customers and the customers' friends know your name.
- **Distribution** (speakeasies): supplying pays steady ($50-150/wk per room); OWNING pays triple and needs a front, a floor manager, protection from yourself, and the cops' schedule. The city holds hundreds, from blind pigs to the Emerald Room.

## Dope - the small trade with the loudest ending

A tenth of the money and triple the shame: in 1926 even hard outfits publicly shun this - which is why the one organized network operates unexamined (secrets.md #1). The GM plays it dark and honest: addiction has faces, and they recur.

- **Opium** (legacy): two aging dens by the harbor, tolerated for a fee, dying with their customers. Entry is inheritance, not violence - the operators want a protector, not a partner.
- **Morphine and heroin** (the pharmacy network): war veterans and prescription pads; crooked pharmacists divert legally-made supply. Sorrentino's "Doc" Gatti runs the ONLY organized network - through pharmacies City Hall licenses. Steady $1,500/wk at scale, a rounding error next to booze - and pulling the thread of why it is never raided finds the machine. Cole wants it more than any still; the papers would burn whoever holds it.
- **Cocaine** (nightlife): powder through club washrooms and bandstands, sold by staff who ARE the marketing. High margin, tiny volume, and every arrest talks.
- **A boss's choice**: entering this trade at all is an IDENTITY decision the whole city reads. Refusing it costs nothing today and buys standing; the player who takes it inherits its customers, its shame, and eventually its owner's attention.

## Gambling

- **Policy/numbers**: pennies at volume, the neighborhood's own lottery. Northside's banks run the honest-for-the-trade version downtown envies; a downtown boss horning in on Northside starts a war the pack prices honestly. Running your own book in your own wards: $300-900/wk, needs runners (a unit) and a bank that always pays winners VISIBLY.
- **The wire** (horse rooms): race results by telegraph minutes early; the wire service is a monopoly worth more than any casino - whoever holds the city's wire taxes every horse room in it. Currently: a Brauer subsidiary, lightly guarded, deeply lawyered.
- **Card rooms and dice** (pool halls, hotel floors): $100-400/wk each, cheat-proofing is the labor; casino night on a lake boat (out past jurisdiction) is the prestige play - $2,000+ a sailing, weather and hijackers permitting.
- **Slots and punchboards**: one-armed bandits in cigar stores, candy shops, and station bars - $10-25/wk per machine, and PLACEMENT is the racket (the route man, the splits, the shopkeeper who "agrees" to host). Boring, steady, scalable, and the first thing reform mayors photograph being smashed.
- **The fix** (prizefights, wire-assisted horses): buying a fighter costs less than a truck and pays like a shipment IF the bets are spread thin enough not to move the line. A fix that leaks reprices every bookmaker's trust in the city, including yours.

## Vice

Brothels, taxi-dance halls, clip joints, the badger game. Real money and the fastest route to enemies with nothing left to lose. The GM plays the people in this trade as PEOPLE - workers with drives, debts, and exits wanted - and lets the player learn what kind of boss they are by how they run or refuse it. Refusing it entirely is a viable identity the city notices ("the 9th Ward boss doesn't run girls" travels).

## The rackets

- **Protection**: the foundation. A corner business pays $10-40/wk to be left alone - by you, and, the actual product, by everyone else. Collection needs a unit; RAISING rates raises defection and heat together. A protected block that genuinely IS protected (burglars beaten, drunks moved along) pays happier and votes your way later.
- **The juice** (loan sharking): $5 back for every $4, weekly. Dockworkers before payday, grocers after a bad month, gamblers always. The book compounds quietly into the real product: PEOPLE WHO OWE YOU - a clerk's ledger access, a juror's vote, a cop's blind eye, all cheaper than bribes because the debt walked in the door on its own.
- **Commodity cartels** (ice, coal, milk routes): "stabilize" delivery prices, tax every wagon, break the independents' wheels. Invisible to the papers, felt by every kitchen in the city - the quiet fortune and the slow hatred.
- **Labor**: locals, hiring halls, strike insurance - paid by both sides if played right. Entry is a union man who owes you (Teddy's whole world). The Carricks consider the waterfront locals THEIRS by blood.
- **The association scam**: the "Calder Bay Grocers' Benevolent Association" - dues, enforced membership, price-fixing. Slower, cleaner protection with a lawyer's face.
- **Fencing and cargo**: everything that walks off the docks needs a buyer who asks nothing. Warehouse + ledgers + discretion: $400-1,000/wk at scale.
- **Yegg work** (safes, payrolls, stolen securities): a good boxman is an artist who tours; hosting his crew for a week costs hospitality and buys a cut of a payroll safe or a bond haul - which then needs LAUNDERING, and hot securities burn amateurs. High score, high heat, zero recurring revenue: robbery is an event, never a business.
- **Torch jobs** (insurance): failing businesses pay a third of the policy to burn clean. Steady money in a bad economy, and the single fastest way to kill bystanders and become the thing even other outfits inform on. Arson has the era's shortest distance between money and monstrous.
- **Counterfeit**: a printer, plates, and patience. Fake bills leak through your own businesses first - Treasury men make Cole look friendly.
- **Kidnapping for ransom**: the era's ugliest boom trade. The pack does not forbid it; the world prices it - permanent heat, permanent grudges, and a name mothers curse. Some doors close forever behind it.
- **Murder for hire**: selling the quiet crew's services makes you money and makes you a WITNESS to everyone who hired you. The smart version is refusing loudly and remembering who asked.

## The legal ladder - going legit, and looking it

Every dirty dollar wants to become a clean one; the two states are DIFFERENT and the fiction tests both (Running at Scale, fronts):

- **Fronts** (appearing legal): laundries, cafes, printers, cab companies - businesses whose books can plausibly hold your money. A front's quality is its plausibility; the ratio heat reads the citywide picture.
- **Real businesses** (being legal): near-beer, real estate, trucking, eventually banking. Real profits, real payrolls, real votes. Brauer's whole endgame. A player can genuinely convert - it takes YEARS of in-game discipline, an accountant better than the tax men, and surviving the friends who liked you dirty.
- **Charity and standing**: soup kitchens, church roofs, ball clubs, Christmas turkeys. Cheap, sincere or not, and the machine counts the goodwill in votes. The neighborhood knowing your name fondly is armor no gun provides.
- **Politics**: aldermen rent by the month; owning one outright takes his secret. The mayoralty itself is the deepest play in the pack - the machine can be joined, bought into, beaten at the polls, or replaced. See arcs.md: the Election of 1927.

## People infrastructure (what crews are FOR)

Specialists gate trades: an ACCOUNTANT (covers ratio, finds skimmers, commits the tax evasion that saves you or sinks you), a LAWYER (bail, judges, juror lists, licenses, the association scam), a CHEMIST (cutting plant, still quality), a WIRE MAN (telegraph, phones, the operators who listen), FIXERS (a cop, a clerk, a reporter, a pharmacist - each a named person with a price and a limit). Every specialist is an NPC with drives, a file, and a breaking point - infrastructure that can be arrested, bought away, or turned.
